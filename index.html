Opacity

Rethinking Gender & Sexuality in African Histories

This digital project explores how African gender and sexual identities have been interpreted, misinterpreted, and politicized through colonial history, Western scholarship, and contemporary identity frameworks.

Drawing on Keguro Macharia’s concept of opacity, this project asks whether the past should always be explained, or whether some histories resist modern categories.

Studying Africa

African scholars have long questioned the ethics of how Africa has been studied. Knowledge about African societies has often been produced through Western academic institutions, where African people are treated as objects of study rather than producers of theory.

This raises important questions: Who has authority to interpret African sexualities? Who benefits from that knowledge? And whose voices are recognized within academic discourse?

The Myth of "Un-African"

Across contemporary political debates, homosexuality is often described as “un-African.” However, many laws criminalizing same-sex relationships originated during European colonial rule.

These legal systems were imposed across African territories and remain embedded within modern national legal frameworks. As a result, sexuality politics today cannot be understood without examining colonial histories.

Colonial Law

Postcolonial States

Modern Politics

Opacity: Letting the Past Remain Complex

Keguro Macharia argues that scholars should grant the past “the right to opacity.” Rather than forcing historical experiences into modern identity categories, we must recognize that past gender and sexual formations may not translate into contemporary frameworks.
